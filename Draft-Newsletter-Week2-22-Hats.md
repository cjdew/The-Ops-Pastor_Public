# The 22 hats I wear

Last week I introduced myself and this newsletter. This week I want to get specific.

I keep saying I carry executive responsibility across "multiple domains." That's vague. So I sat down and actually mapped every distinct operational function that runs through me at Mosaic Wadsworth. Not categories — functions. Things that, at a larger organization, would each have a dedicated person or team.

Here's the list.

1. Network architecture and WiFi management
2. Server and cloud infrastructure (Azure)
3. Device management and MDM
4. Internal application development
5. Help desk and staff tech support
6. Security policy and access control
7. Vendor management (technology)
8. Budget management and financial planning
9. Giving analytics and donor follow-up workflows
10. Accounts payable and expense management
11. Audit prep and compliance
12. Staff onboarding and offboarding
13. Policy documentation and employee handbook
14. Background checks and HR compliance
15. Facilities work orders and maintenance
16. Vendor management (facilities)
17. Safety compliance and building operations
18. Internal communications infrastructure
19. Staff platform administration (PCO, M365, Slack)
20. Knowledge base architecture and deployment
21. Data analytics and reporting (engagement scoring, dashboards)
22. Organizational systems design and workflow architecture

Twenty-two. Some weeks I touch all of them. Most weeks I touch at least half.

## Why the number matters

Listing them isn't self-pity. It's a diagnostic tool.

When I first did this exercise, two things became immediately clear. First, the reason I felt overwhelmed wasn't poor time management — it was that the scope of the role is structurally too broad for one person. That's different. One is a personal failing. The other is an organizational design problem.

Second, once I could see all 22 functions in one place, I could start making real decisions about them. Which ones need my direct involvement? Which ones can be systematized? Which ones can be delegated? Which ones am I holding onto because I'm the only one who knows how they work — and what would need to be true for someone else to take them over?

That last question is the one that changes things.

## The uncomfortable math

Here's a rough capacity model. Assume a 50-hour work week (realistic for this role). Twenty-two functions across 50 hours is about 2.3 hours per function per week. That's not enough time to do any of them well. It's barely enough time to keep them from falling apart.

In practice, the time isn't distributed evenly. A few functions absorb most of the hours — tech support, financial management, and platform administration eat time because they're reactive. The strategic functions — systems design, analytics, knowledge architecture — get whatever's left, which is usually not much.

This is why mapping the full scope matters. Without it, the reactive work wins every time, and the work that actually builds organizational capacity keeps getting pushed.

## What I did about it

Three things.

First, I categorized each function by whether it requires my expertise, my authority, or just my availability. The "just my availability" functions are the first delegation targets. Password resets, basic work order routing, routine vendor communication — these don't need an executive pastor. They need a process and a person.

Second, I started building systems to replace my involvement where possible. The knowledge base deployment pipeline replaced me manually updating WordPress. The work order system replaced me being the person who knows what needs fixing. The engagement scoring runs automatically every morning instead of me pulling reports.

Third, I got honest about the functions I was holding because I liked them, not because I was the only option. That's a harder conversation, but it matters.

## Your turn

How many hats do you wear? I'm genuinely asking. Reply to this email with your number. Don't just guess — actually list them.

The point isn't to compare. The point is to see the full picture. Most solo ops people undercount by at least five, because some of the hats feel like "just part of the job" rather than distinct functions.

Once you can see them all, you can start making decisions about them. That's where things start to shift.
