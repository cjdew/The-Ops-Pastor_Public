# LinkedIn content plan — weeks 1-4

Cadence: 2-3 posts per week. Short (150-300 words), hook-driven, repurposed from newsletter content. End with a question or clear call to engage.

---

## Week 1 (April 6-12): Launch week

**Post 1 — Monday: The announcement**

Last Wednesday I configured a firewall rule, fixed a finance integration, stood up a decision log, debugged a React component, approved a facilities work order, and helped a volunteer reset their password.

That was before lunch.

My title is Executive Pastor. My actual job is carrying executive responsibility for operations, finance, technology, facilities, HR, communications, and data analytics at a church of 700.

I've been doing this for 15 years across three churches. I've built systems, broken systems, and rebuilt them. And the resources available for people in this role are mostly terrible.

So I'm starting something. It's called The Ops Pastor.

One newsletter a week. Real systems. Real decisions. Real tools. No fluff.

If half your job exists because nobody else had time to pick it up — this is for you.

Link in comments.

**Post 2 — Wednesday: The hook**

How many operational domains do you personally manage?

I counted mine last week. I stopped at 22.

Not categories. Functions. Things that would each have a dedicated person at a larger organization.

Network architecture. Budget management. Staff onboarding. Facilities maintenance. Platform administration. Data analytics. And 16 more.

Most solo ops people undercount by at least five, because some hats feel like "just part of the job."

What's your number?

**Post 3 — Friday: The insight**

The reason most church ops people feel overwhelmed isn't poor time management.

It's that the scope of the role is structurally too broad for one person.

Those are different problems. One is personal. The other is organizational design.

You can't time-manage your way out of 22 functions in a 50-hour week. That's 2.3 hours per domain. That's not enough time to do any of them well.

The fix isn't working harder. The fix is mapping the full scope, then making real decisions about which functions need your expertise, which ones need your authority, and which ones just need your availability.

The third category is where delegation starts.

## Week 2 (April 13-19): Origin story week

**Post 1 — Tuesday: Origin story excerpt**

Nobody plans to become a one-person operations department.

I've spent 15 years in church planting across three churches — admin, assistant pastor, lead pastor. The scope always expands the same way.

Finance shows up because someone needs to understand the budget. HR shows up because someone needs onboarding paperwork. IT shows up because someone needs email accounts. Facilities shows up because the HVAC breaks and you're the one who called the vendor.

Each responsibility makes sense on its own. Put together, it's a full operations department with one employee.

I wrote about how this role gets built and what I'm doing about it. Link in comments.

**Post 2 — Thursday: The integration point**

The hardest part of running church operations isn't the individual functions.

It's the integration layer.

Making sure finance, facilities, HR, tech, and communications don't operate as disconnected silos — that's where the real complexity lives.

Nobody talks about this part. There's no job title for it. There's no training for it. But it's the difference between an organization that runs smoothly and one that just barely holds together.

**Post 3 — Saturday: The contrarian**

Most church operations resources fall into three categories:

Too churchy. ("God-honoring spreadsheets!")
Too corporate. ("Implement a stakeholder alignment matrix.")
Too shallow. ("Five tips for organizing your desk.")

Church ops people deserve better than all three.

The work is real. The problems are real. The systems need to be real too.

50 hours a week.
22 operational functions.
2.3 hours per function.

That's not enough time to do any of them well. It's barely enough to keep them from breaking.

## Week 4 (April 27-May 3): Campfire teardown week

**Post 1 — Tuesday: The Slack question**

What does your church pay for Slack?

We were paying thousands a year for a team of 10. For a communication tool.

So I started building a self-hosted alternative. It's not done yet — I'm at about 70%, waiting on Apple developer certification for push notifications.

But the process of evaluating, deciding, and building has been one of the most useful exercises I've done. New teardown post is up this week.

**Post 2 — Thursday: Build vs buy**

When should a church build internal tools instead of buying SaaS?

My framework is simple. Build when:
— The SaaS tool costs more than the infrastructure to replace it
— Nothing off the shelf does what you need
— You have the technical skill and the will to maintain it
— The tool touches data you need to control

Don't build when:
— You're the only one who can maintain it
— The problem is process, not tooling
— Building it is more interesting to you than the actual outcome

That last one is the hardest to admit.

**Post 3 — Saturday: The cost reveal**

We run 8 internal applications on Azure for under $150/month.

Facilities work orders. IT tickets. HR records. Inventory. Attendance tracking. Giving analytics. Communications. And a data dashboard.

Most churches pay more than that for a single SaaS tool they barely use.

I'm writing up the full tech stack with costs next week. Everything we run, what it costs, and what I'd cut.

## General LinkedIn rules

- Never start with "I'm excited to announce" or similar
- No emojis
- Short paragraphs (1-2 sentences)
- End with a question when possible
- Link in comments, not in the post body (better reach)
- Don't post on Sundays
- Engage with 5-10 comments on other church ops content per week
